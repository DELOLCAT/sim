A few years ago, I lived in California. Back then, it was (and still is) going through a nasty drought, causing severe water shortages and seasons of wildfires. Anyone trying to fix it would have to deal with a messy mix of environmental, economic, and political systems... all of which came together to create the perfect lack-of-rain-let-alone-a-storm.

Point is, the world is filled with complex systems. They may seem impossible to change or comprehend, but I believe all of us can — and must — learn to think in systems.

That’s where simulations come in! It’s easier to understand a system if you can see the system, or better yet, play with the system. So today, I’d like to share with you some simple simulations of the world... made in emoji. 🌲🌲🌲

A Tiny Forest For Tiny Trees

As it turns out, forest firefighters already use computer simulations to tackle wildfires, in real time. And it’s not just foresters. With the help of simulations, a growing group of scientists from different disciplines are realizing something strange: all complex systems have things in common.

So, in a way... Financial crises are like forest fires. Terrorist groups are like termite colonies. Pandemics are like a GIF everyone keeps sharing even though it’s already two days old. Since you can learn some core lessons about all systems from any system, let’s stick with our forest sim, and add one more rule:

🌲→🔥: Trees can get struck by lightning & catch on fire.

Sims can help explain a system, but they're also great for exploring systems, asking “What If?...” Like, what do you think will happen now, if you increased the growth rate? (Don’t worry, this question isn’t to judge or grade you, it’s solely for you to express your prediction)

🌲📈→🔥📈→🌲📉→🔥📉→🌲📈⟲

This is thinking in systems. See, we’re so used to thinking in linear cause-and-effect, A-changes-B. But most of the world is filled with what scientists call feedback loops, where A-changes-B-changes-A-changes-B, and so on.

The first kind of feedback loop is the reinforcing loop, where more gets you even more. For example, in our simulation, fire creates more fire. This kind of loop leads to exponential growth.

more leads to more: 🔥📈→🔥📈

The second kind is the balancing loop, where more leads to less. It’s harder to find these kinds of loops, but there’s a trick: think of a reinforcing loop. Then, think of what stops that loop from going to infinity. That’s your balancing loop. In our sim, fire makes more fire, but it can’t go on forever, because it'll run out of trees to burn. This kind of loop creates cyclical ups-and-downs.

more leads to less: 🔥📈→🌲📉→🔥📉

This is why we’re so bad at handling complex issues. Everyone’s looking for the root cause, but if causality is not linear, but loopy, then there is no root cause!

But that doesn’t mean we can’t still find ways to deal with complex problems. Again, simulations can help. In this next bit, you’re going to save the forest by killing trees:

A Forest Where You’re In Charge Now, Somehow, I Guess

Yup, you contained fire and helped more trees grow, by killing trees. And yes, forest firefighters actually do this! They make “firebreaks” by removing small trees, underbrush, and other flammable shtuff, to prevent wildfires from spreading through. This breaks the reinforcing loop of 🔥📈→🔥📈, which also reduces the balancing loop of 🌲📈→🔥📈→🌲📉.

And that is how you change a system. Not by fiddling with numbers, or fighting the symptoms, but by changing the core loops.

Now that you’ve gotten the hang of systems, let’s do one last forest sim! A forest wouldn’t be an ecosystem with just one species, so let’s simulate a forest with these two plants:
